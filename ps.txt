PROBLEM STATEMENT
JusticeGrid
AI-Augmented Legal Intelligence for India's Undertrial Crisis
SOC 2
PROBLEM OVERVIEW
India's criminal justice system confines 4,34,302 of its 5,73,220
prisoners without conviction - the largest undertrial population in the
world. Many of them are not there because they are dangerous. They are
there because the system lacks the intelligence layer to identify that they
should be free.
The law already provides the tools: Section 479 BNSS mandates bail if
half the maximum sentence has been served. NALSA guarantees free
legal representation for all undertrials. Under-Trial Review Committees
are legally required. But no system connects these legal rights to the
individual people they apply to - at the speed and scale needed.
JusticeGrid is a real-time legal intelligence layer for NALSA panel
lawyers, DLSA paralegals, and UTRC coordinators. It is not a
replacement for lawyers. It is not an automated bail filing system.
The AI's job is to make a single paralegal with 200 cases as effective as
a team of 10. It monitors every active case for eligibility events, surfaces
the right case to the right person at the right moment, and explains
eligibility in plain language the paralegal can act on. The human is
always responsible for every decision.
Expected Features:
Every submission must address all ten pillars. These are not independent features - they form a
complete system. A submission that nails Pillars 1-3 but ignores Pillars 6-10 is building a prototype,
not a deployable product.

Pillar 01 : Multi-Source Legal Data Fusion & Eligibility Intelligence
•Build a unified data layer that ingests and normalises case data from 25 state eCourts
portals, NJDG, FIR text, court cause lists, and prison records - each with different
schemas, languages, update frequencies, and access controls - treating this
heterogeneity as a permanent operating condition.
•Compute Section 479 BNSS eligibility dynamically per case: extract charge sections
from unstructured multilingual FIR text, map to maximum sentences, distinguish firsttime offenders (⅓ threshold) from regular accused (½ threshold), handle multiple
charges and IPC-to-BNSS transitional ambiguity, and never auto-apply the most
favourable charge.
•Re-evaluate every active case daily as detention duration updates, track whether an
eligibility window is about to expire without action, and escalate accordingly - eligibility
is not a one-time check.
Pillar 02 : Case Complexity Classification & Legal Aid Prioritisation
•Rank every case in the paralegal's queue by a composite priority score across four
signals: eligibility status, time sensitivity (days to next hearing, days since last action,
days until window closes), action availability (lawyer assigned, documents present),
and court behaviour score (historical adjournment rate for this court-judge-case-type
combination).
•Address conflict cases explicitly - a case with a potential life imprisonment count
must be flagged for lawyer review rather than surfaced as auto-eligible; a case with a 3-
day hearing at a court with 89% adjournment probability must be ranked below a newly
eligible case with a real upcoming window.
•Calibrated uncertainty is mandatory: every output must show its confidence level and
explain its reasoning. An overconfident system that surfaces incorrect bail eligibility
wastes court time and destroys paralegal trust.
Pillar 03 : Paralegal & Family-Facing Interface Under Real Conditions
•Paralegal workstation view: case queue ranked by priority score with a single-line
reason per case, one-click expansion to charges and recommended next action,
hearing prep brief in the paralegal's language, and full offline mode for prison visits
with sync on reconnection

•Family notification layer: WhatsApp/SMS case status updates in the family's auto-detected
language, plain-language hearing outcome translations, opt-in only, and an escalation trigger
when no notification has been sent in 45 days despite an action-relevant event. Never legal
advice. Never predictions.
•UTRC coordinator dashboard: prison-level view of Section 479-eligible undertrials by charge
category and detention duration, hearing schedule conflicts, action-needed queue, district
comparison, and one-click NALSA quarterly report export.
Pillar 04 : Systemic Pattern Intelligence Across the Case Corpus
•Surface systemic insights from the case corpus: which charge sections drive the longest
undertrial detention, which specific courts have the worst bail-decision intervals for eligible
cases, which prisons have the highest density of eligible-but-unserved undertrials, and which
surety amounts are blocking bail execution correlated with local poverty indicators.
•Feed every outcome (bail granted, adjournment, surety blocked) back into the eligibility
engine, adjournment predictor, and complexity classifier so the system becomes measurably
more accurate over time.
•Privacy is a hard architectural constraint: no personally identifiable information about any
prisoner may be retained beyond active case management needs. Learning happens from
anonymised patterns - enforced structurally in the data architecture with a visible data
lifecycle diagram.
Pillar 05 : Ethical Architecture, Failure Modes & Accountability Design
•Every eligibility assessment must be logged immutably with: data sources and timestamps
used, legal reasoning chain, confidence level, and paralegal response (acted on / overrode /
flagged). If a bail application was not filed for an eligible prisoner, supervisors must be able to
reconstruct exactly what the system said and when.
•Each failure mode must have a specific, designed, graceful degradation response - not a
generic error message. Required cases: eCourts API downtime (mark cases as stale, block
auto-escalation), FIR parsing below confidence threshold (flag for mandatory lawyer review,
never include silently in eligible queue), name/identity mismatch (never auto-merge, flag for
human resolution).
•Misuse architecture: role-based access so no single role has bulk access to prisoner data
without an audit trail; no bulk export without DLSA supervisor approval and logged
justification; DPDPA 2023 compliance structural - data localisation, right to erasure, and
deletion triggers baked into the data architecture.
Pillar 06 : Multilingual NLP & Language Justice Engine
•Extract charge sections from FIR text written in Hindi, English, Marathi, Bengali, Telugu,
Tamil, Kannada, Gujarati, Odia, and Punjabi - where sections appear in narrative prose
rather than structured fields, OCR errors are common, and scripts are often mixed within a
single document.
•Generate plain-language case status summaries in 10+ Indian languages, with legal
terminology translated not word-for-word but for comprehension by someone with no legal
literacy - and with a human review pathway for quality assurance in every language.
•Handle name normalisation across transliterations (the same name may appear in 8
different forms across state portals) with a conservative merge policy: if uncertain whether
two records belong to the same person, flag for human review - never auto-merge.
Pillar 07 : Bail Surety & Financial Access Intelligence
•Identify all cases where bail was granted more than 14 days ago but no surety has been
executed - the immediate intervention targets - and cross-reference the surety amount
against median daily wage and MGNREGA rates in the accused's home district.
•Surface cases where the surety amount exceeds 90 days of local median income as
strong indicators for a Section 440 surety reduction application; auto-generate a surety
reduction application brief for lawyer review in such cases.
•Map systemic patterns: which courts set surety amounts that are systematically
unaffordable for their district's income profile - and feed these findings into the policy
intelligence layer.
Pillar 08 : Adjournment Prediction & Court Behaviour Modelling
•Predict adjournment probability (0-100% with confidence interval) for each upcoming
hearing using: historical adjournment rate for this court-judge-case-type combination,
number of consecutive prior adjournments on this case, day of week, proximity to court
vacation periods, and whether the prosecution has filed its charge sheet.
•Use adjournment predictions to reprioritise the paralegal's hearing-day schedule: if
adjournment probability exceeds 75% and another case has a time-sensitive action,
surface that signal clearly.
•Surface pattern alerts to UTRC when a court has adjourned eligible cases 20+ times in a
quarter. The model must show its uncertainty prominently - predictions are inputs to
judgment, not instructions.
Pillar 09 : Federated Intelligence Across DLSAs
•Federated learning architecture: local models train on district-level data; only model
weights : not individual records - are shared centrally. Differential privacy guarantees must
ensure individual case records cannot be reverse-engineered from aggregate weights.
Asynchronous federation allows DLSAs with low connectivity to contribute when available.
•Operationally: a DLSA with no data engineering capacity must be able to configure and
deploy their local node with a one-page setup guide - no developer required. Districts that
contribute more data get more accurate predictions for their own district.
•NALSA governance layer showing which DLSAs are participating, data quality contributed,
and model accuracy improvement produced. A DLSA that withdraws must be able to have
their contributed weights removed from the global model - architecturally enforced, not just
a policy promise.
Pillar 10 : Voice-First Access for Zero-Literacy Users
•WhatsApp voice message delivery of case status updates in the family's dialect, with an
IVR fallback for feature phones - family calls a number, enters the case reference, and
receives a voice briefing with no smartphone required.
•Dialect differentiation within languages (Hindi for Bhojpuri-speaking UP is not the same as
Hindi for Rajasthani-speaking families), consistent plain-speech equivalents for legal terms
('remand', 'bail', 'charge sheet', 'next hearing'), and an active listening mode where the family
member can say 'what does that mean?' after any update.
•Safe escalation: if the family member appears confused or distressed, the interface offers
to connect them to the DLSA helpline. The voice interface never gives legal advice, never
predicts outcomes, never asks for personal information beyond the case reference, and
never stores voice input beyond the session.
